A plain-language guide to the Shiftwork Solutions schedule library: what each schedule family is, how to read the cards, and what the numbers mean. Use it alongside the **Master Schedule Library** (all families in one file) and the **Schedule Catalog** (the interactive, ratable version for Survey in a Box).

## The basis for every calculation

All year-level statistics assume a standard year of **364 days / 52 weeks**. Every schedule in the library keeps its own repeating cycle, and the numbers are worked out from one full cycle and then scaled to the year.

## Shift lengths in the library

The library covers three shift lengths:

- **8-hour** — five-day office patterns and their extensions to continuous (24/5) coverage.
- **10-hour** — four 10-hour days for a 40-hour week (the "4×10" family), used for weekday coverage and rotation.
- **12-hour** — the workhorse of continuous (24/7) operations; two shifts a day, day and night.

## How to read a schedule card

Each schedule is drawn as a grid, one row per week of the cycle, one column per day.

- **Gold cell (D12 / D10 / D8)** — a day shift.
- **Navy cell (N12 / N10)** — a night shift.
- **Slate cell with an hour number** — a generic "basic" shift before day/night is assigned.
- **Light-gray cell** — a day off.
- **Green header row** — the days of the week.
- **Red divider line** — separates one crew from another in a multi-crew schedule.
- **Dashed red outline** — marks a cell that has been deliberately moved or that rotates (used in the "Modified" and rotating patterns).
- **Spec chips** — quick facts under the title: shift length, cycle length, average hours per week, and coverage.

**Day-start convention:** continuous 24/7 patterns are drawn **Sunday-start**; weekday-driven patterns (Six-Day, 10-Hour) are drawn **Monday-start** so the work block reads left-to-right with the weekend at the end.

## The key statistics, defined

Each schedule in the catalog carries these figures, calculated from the point of view of one worker in a crew:

- **Scheduled work days / year** — days actually worked, scaled from the cycle to 364 days.
- **Scheduled days off / year** — the remainder of the 364.
- **Average hours worked / week** — cycle hours divided by the weeks in the cycle.
- **Full weekends off / year** — weekends where *both* Saturday and Sunday are off.
- **Half weekends off / year** — weekends where exactly *one* of Saturday or Sunday is off (a "split" weekend).
- **Most days of work in a row** — the longest unbroken run of work days in the cycle.
- **Fewest days of work in a row** — the shortest run of work days in the cycle.
- **Key feature** — two or three sentences on what makes the schedule distinctive and what the trade-off is.

## Crew concepts

- **Basic pattern** — the underlying on/off rhythm, before crews and day/night are assigned. It's the building block the other versions are made from.
- **Fixed** — each crew stays on one shift forever. A day crew never works nights. Simple and predictable, but night crews are permanent.
- **Rotating** — crews move between days and nights on a set rhythm (fast, medium, or slow). Night work is shared across everyone rather than falling on permanent night crews.
- **Front / back halves** — in many 24/7 patterns the week is split into a front block and a back (mirror-image) block; two crews interlock to cover all seven days.
- **Stagger ("N weeks apart")** — to make two crews cover continuously without overlap, the partner crew starts its cycle a set number of weeks later. The exact offset depends on the pattern: the 3-on/3-off partner starts **3 weeks** later, the 4-on/4-off partner **4 weeks** later, and the 4-3 back-half crew starts mid-week. This offset is what makes coverage interlock cleanly.
- **Complementary coverage** — when one crew is off, its partner is on, so exactly the right number of people are present every day.

## The "Modified" idea (clean weekends)

Some continuous patterns split weekends — you work Saturday but are off Sunday, or the reverse. The **Modified** versions relocate a single Saturday shift within the cycle so that every weekend is *whole*: either both days off or both days worked, never split. The cost is one slightly shorter and one slightly longer work run; the weekly average never changes.

- **3-on/3-off Modified** — trades the split weekends for a mix of full weekends off and full worked weekends; work runs become one 2-in-a-row and one 4-in-a-row.
- **4-on/4-off Modified** — even cleaner: four full weekends off in a row, then four fully-worked weekends; work runs become one 3-in-a-row and one 5-in-a-row.

## The families at a glance

1. **5-Day / 24-5** — 8-hour office foundation and its extension to five-day continuous coverage.
2. **10-Hour** — four 10-hour days for a 40-hour week: a basic Mon–Thu, a two-crew stagger (peak mid-week staffing), and a five-sub-crew rotation that shares the long weekend fairly.
3. **2-2-3** — the classic 12-hour every-other-weekend-off pattern.
4. **5 and 2** — 12-hour pattern built on long on/off blocks.
5. **4-3** — 12-hour, 24/7 with four crews; the same days each week plus a fourth every other week.
6. **Six-Day Coverage** — 10- and 12-hour patterns for Monday–Saturday operations.
7. **3-on / 3-off** — three on, three off; basic, rapid rotation, and fixed 24/7 versions.
8. **3-on / 3-off Modified** — the clean-weekend version.
9. **4-on / 4-off** — four on, four off; basic, rapid and medium rotation, and fixed 24/7.
10. **4-on / 4-off Modified** — the clean-weekend version.
11. **DuPont** — the classic four-crew 12-hour reference pattern.

## Glossary

- **Cycle** — the number of weeks after which the schedule repeats on the same weekdays.
- **Coverage** — how many crews are present on a given shift; "24/7" means one crew on days and one on nights every day of the week.
- **24/5** — continuous coverage Monday through Friday only.
- **Realign** — because work cycles (6-day, 8-day, etc.) don't divide evenly into a 7-day week, a schedule can take several weeks before your days off land on the same weekdays again; that span is the cycle length.
