Key Feature
The 4-3 is a 12-hour pattern built from two complementary halves of the week. You work the same three days every week and add a fourth every other week, which balances the schedule to a steady 42 hours. It is the foundation the fixed and rotating 24/7 versions are built on.
How It Works
1
The pattern

The basic card shows one half of the week on its own — three 12-hour shifts to start the week, then four the following week. A second, mirror-image half covers the back of the week; together they blanket all seven days.

2
Your two weeks

Week 1 you work three 12-hour shifts (Sunday, Monday, Tuesday) and are off the rest. Week 2 you work four (Sunday through Wednesday). Across the fortnight that is seven shifts — 84 hours, or 42 a week — then it repeats.

Key Feature
The 4-3 keeps every crew on the same days at the start of each week, adding a fourth day every other week to balance the hours. It delivers true 24/7 coverage with four crews and a steady 42-hour weekly average — but because each crew works either every Saturday or every Sunday, full weekends off aren't part of this pattern.
How It Works
1
Where the crews go

Four crews cover the operation — two on days, two on nights. Within each shift one crew takes the front of the week and one takes the back. Day-Front and Night-Front begin on Sunday; Day-Back and Night-Back begin on Wednesday. The two halves interlock so exactly one day crew and one night crew are on the floor every day.

2
Your cycle, week by week

Say you're on Day-Front. Week 1 you work Sunday, Monday, Tuesday, then off through Saturday. Week 2 you work Sunday through Wednesday, then off through Saturday. Every two weeks you put in seven 12-hour shifts — three one week, four the next — averaging 42 hours.

Key Feature
The rotating 4-3 gives you the same 42-hour week and 24/7 coverage as the fixed version, but instead of staying on one shift each crew spends two weeks on days and two weeks on nights. It shares the night work evenly across the workforce rather than concentrating it on two permanent night crews.
How It Works
1
Where the crews go

Four crews cover the operation, each tied to one half of the week — a front pair and a back pair. Rather than one crew owning days and another owning nights, every crew alternates: two weeks of day shifts, then two weeks of night shifts, on a four-week loop. At any moment two crews are on days and two on nights, so coverage never changes.

2
Your four weeks

Say you're a front-half crew. Weeks 1–2 you work the front of the week on day shift; weeks 3–4 you work the same front-of-week days on night shift; then back to days. Your weekdays don't move — only whether they're a D12 or an N12 — so you face just one shift change every two weeks.
