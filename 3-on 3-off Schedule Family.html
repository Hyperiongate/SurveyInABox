Key Feature
Three 12-hour shifts on, three off, repeating. It is one of the simplest continuous patterns to understand and averages 42 hours a week, but because the 6-day rhythm slides against the 7-day week your days off land on different weekdays each time — it takes six weeks before your calendar repeats.
How It Works
1
The pattern

There are no fixed weekdays — you work three days, take three, and repeat. Shown on a calendar the block of three walks steadily later each week, so a weekend off this cycle becomes a mid-week break the next.

2
Your six weeks

Follow any single row and you'll see three 12s together, then three blank days, over and over. After six weeks (42 days) the pattern lines back up with the same weekdays. Over a year that is 182 days worked at 42 hours a week.

Key Feature
This is the 3-on/3-off rhythm with day and night work folded in: three day shifts, three off, three night shifts, three off. You flip between days and nights quickly, which spreads the night work evenly but asks your body to adjust often. It realigns to the calendar every twelve weeks.
How It Works
1
The pattern

One crew runs the whole cycle: three 12-hour day shifts, three off, three 12-hour night shifts, three off, then repeat. Days and nights alternate every block, so no one is stuck permanently on nights.

2
Your twelve weeks

You work a block of three days, rest three, work three nights, rest three — a twelve-day loop. Because twelve days and seven-day weeks don't divide evenly, it takes twelve weeks to return to the same weekdays. You still average 42 hours a week.

Key Feature
The 3-on/3-off run as a fixed 24/7 operation. Two crews work days and two work nights, and nobody switches between them. The trick is crew placement: to interlock coverage, the partner crew has to start three weeks into the cycle.
How It Works
1
Where the crews go

Four crews cover the clock — two permanently on days, two on nights. Within each shift the two crews are offset so that when one is in its three-on block the other is in its three-off block. Because the 6-day rhythm and the 7-day week don't line up, the partner crew begins its cycle three weeks later, which is what makes coverage interlock cleanly.

2
Your six weeks

Pick a day crew. You work three 12-hour day shifts, take three off, and repeat — never touching nights. Your partner day crew fills every gap you leave. Over six weeks the pattern realigns; over a year you work 182 days at 42 hours a week.
